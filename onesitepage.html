Spooky, creepy spots in Canberra

Blundells Cottage

Blundells Cottage sits on the shores of Lake Burley Griffin, beneath the looming ASIO headquarters, so it's spooky in more ways than one.

According to Montgomery, items move about inside, and some visitors smell burning flesh which was a throwback to the tragic death of a teenage girl more than a century ago.

"It's Flora Blundell, who [died in the late 1800s] … she was ironing a skirt or a dress," he said.

"She was ironing and it caught fire and it burnt her.

"The story of her is items move around a bit and people smell burning flesh.

"People say that her presence is more likely to be felt when there's someone there wearing a necklace, because it was said she was wearing a necklace when she died, or caught fire at least."

Montgomery said alleged phenomena at the cottage mostly extended to "a presence and a smell".

"Although I had a report from 2010 when some people visited the place at night, one of them was looking in a window, and I think they saw a shadow, or a shadow in black get up from a seated position then walk across one of the rooms and turn and face out the window," he said.

"The person then bolted."

The National Museum (and former Canberra Hospital)

By far the newest and least obviously creepy building on this list — the National Museum might seem benign, but according to Montgomery it harbours a secret straight out of The Shining.

"There's an old woman that appears in a bathtub but there's no bath tub in that room … she's been seen a number of times," he said.

While this phenomenon is actually in a building a short distance from the Museum, Montgomery said all the alleged happenings appeared linked to the old Canberra Hospital, which used to stand on the site.

"There are numerous stories there of a ghostly matron that seemed to be attached to the old hospital," he said.

"There's another one... a young boy who seems to be dressed in 1940s-like clothes."

Hotel Kurrajong

Hotel Kurrajong has been home to many political figures over the years, none more prominent than Prime Minister Ben Chiffley.

One evening in 1951, the then-Prime Minister suffered a fatal heart attack while working in his room — and some say a part of him never left.

"Is it possible that … his being has sort of been absorbed into that room itself," Montgomery said.

"He is reported as standing at the windows looking towards Parliament House."

While Montgomery does believe in ghosts, he describes himself as "highly sceptical".

"Too many people are too quick to say 'oh it's a ghost'," he said.

"If you take the example of castles or mansions in England, and they're quite creepy old places … and you have an expectation that something's going to happen."

National Film and Sound Archive

Described by some as "one of Australia's most haunted buildings", the National Film and Sound Archive used to be home to the Australian Institute of Anatomy.

Its basement contained a morgue, and skeletons used to be on display for attendees to inspect.

When I visited last year, I was almost convinced when a TV light I was carrying mysteriously went out (I later learned there was a logical explanation).

Others have reported being pinned against walls, or seeing childlike faces peering from behind grates in the NFSA's theatre.

So there you have it — four of Canberra's spookiest spots.

Of course, whether you choose to believe the stories are folklore, or something more, is up to you.
